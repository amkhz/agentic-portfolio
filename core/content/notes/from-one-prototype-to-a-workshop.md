---
title: From one prototype to a workshop
date: 2026-06-25
kicker: Retro
summary: A look back at how a single Statement-of-Work prototype turned into an AI-native design practice a team now builds inside every day.
---

It started in March with a narrow problem. Feasibility analysts were assembling Statement-of-Work conditions by hand, so I built a small prototype to automate the worst of it. That was the whole scope. One flow, one annoying task, one repo I named onboarding-app before I had any idea what it would turn into.

Then it kept growing past its own borders. By spring I was building the origination-file work: individual-versus-entity eligibility, evidence inspection, a document pipeline, decisions you could actually see the reasoning behind. That was the first real domain modeling. Around the same time I ran an AI-assisted audit of the existing design system, and it flipped how I saw the thing. The system wasn't just something to consume. It was something to diagnose and rebuild.

Enter Kiavi World. The scattered prototypes became a shared home for the team: a four-view dashboard, the doctrine files that hold our intent, and Key, the agent that reads them before it writes a line. The point was never the tooling. It was having a place to work with the team's standards written down, where both people and the AI could follow them.

Then the work reached production. The Forged brand shipped to real borrowers for the first time, which is a different kind of milestone than a polished prototype. Snapshot, the flagship origination tool, matured enough to hand cleanly to an engineer and keep moving. The prototypes stopped being demos of what could be and started being what borrowers actually touch.

The biggest shift was the quietest one: it stopped being my workflow and became a team's. I onboarded a senior designer using the working prototype as the material instead of a deck, and she shipped into the flagship inside two months. I wrote a growth plan for a designer I was mentoring. I ran parallel agent sprints that landed two architecture decisions at once. Brand voice became a skill that writes and checks copy. The design repo got real CI, which almost nobody bothers to do.

It's June, and the workshop is only a few months old, so this is a snapshot and not a finish line. The arc went from "can AI help me make this one screen faster" to "a team has an operating system it builds inside every day." What's next is making it hold its quality without me in the loop on every decision. That's the real test of whether you built infrastructure or just a really good habit.
