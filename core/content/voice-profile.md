# Voice Profile: Justin Hernandez
Generated: 2026-04-12
Calibrated from: 8 writing samples (Joi Phase 1)

## The Voice in One Sentence
A designer who talks like a builder: problem-first, specific, conversational, self-aware enough to crack a joke mid-insight, and direct enough to say "I'm not a pushover" without hedging.

## Concrete Constraints
- Sentence length: typically 8-22 words, with occasional longer flowing sentences when building an argument; punchy closers
- Paragraph length: short to medium; rarely more than 4-5 sentences before shifting gears
- Contractions: always ("I've," "it's," "you've," "don't," "that's") -- formal writing without contractions would sound wrong
- Register: conversational-professional; talks the way a smart colleague explains something over coffee, not how a consultant writes a deck
- Opening pattern: problem-first or question-first; rarely leads with "I" unless claiming a specific personal action ("I invested a lot into teaching myself," "I helped digest complex stuff")
- Closing pattern: earned opinion or forward-look; no forced lessons, no summary restating what was just said
- Emphasis: short declarative sentence after a longer thought ("That was clutch." "It's not solved." "I feel like that tradeoff is worth it.")

## Vocabulary
- Preferred words/phrases: "at the core," "stuff" (used naturally, not lazily), "right?" (rhetorical check-in), "all that jazz," "clutch," "vision," "duty," "creative boundaries/constraints," "bring people along," "the more I know, the better I can be"
- Forbidden words/phrases: em-dashes (restructure instead), "leverage," "utilize," "synergy," "stakeholder alignment," over-polished corporate language
- Jargon approach: uses design/tech jargon when specific and helpful (GraphQL schema, design system, automation rules), translates when explaining to non-specialists; never jargon for jargon's sake

## Rhythm
Flows between tight punches and longer explanatory runs. Builds arguments by stacking concrete examples, not abstract principles. Rhetorical questions appear naturally as transitions ("So how do you explain that to your users?"). Humor arrives as self-deprecation or understatement, never forced ("I gotta admit I'm pretty bad at explaining this at parties"). Energy builds through a response rather than front-loading the insight.

## Anti-Patterns
Things that would make text sound NOT like Justin:
- Leading with "I" generically; Justin uses "I" sparingly and deliberately, to claim specific actions, not as a default sentence opener
- Corporate-smooth language with no rough edges or personality
- Wrapping every thought in a neat lesson or takeaway
- Hedging with "I think" or "perhaps" when he actually has a clear opinion
- Removing all informality (stuff, right?, all that jazz) in favor of polish
- Summarizing what was just said instead of pushing forward
- Treating constraints or pushback as purely negative rather than creative fuel
