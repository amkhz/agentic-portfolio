## The Big Picture {#big-picture}

In 2008, two of the biggest names in international relations theory published a paper about UFOs. Not in a fringe outlet, either. In *Political Theory*, one of political philosophy's flagship journals. Alexander Wendt (Ohio State's Mershon Professor of International Security, one of the most cited IR theorists alive) and Raymond Duvall (Morse-Alumni Professor and chair of political science at Minnesota) aren't fringe figures. That's the point. The credentials are part of the argument, because the claim they're making is the kind fringe figures get dismissed for.

Their starting observation is simple. Scholars fight about everything when it comes to |sovereignty|: who holds it, where it sits, whether it's eroding. But every one of those fights is horizontal, human against human. No one contests the vertical assumption underneath: that sovereignty belongs to humans at all.

> **sovereignty** — The capacity to decide the norm and the exception to it — who rules, on what authority, and when the ordinary rules are suspended.

That unexamined assumption is |anthropocentric sovereignty|, rule "constituted and organized by reference to human beings alone."

> **anthropocentric sovereignty** — The modern assumption that rule is constituted and organized by reference to human beings alone — only humans can be sovereign. A contingent historical achievement, not common sense.

For most of history, gods and Nature shared the job. Modernity expelled them both, then let each one back in on a leash: God now speaks only through human representatives, and Nature returns stripped of subjectivity, just an object. Wendt and Duvall, following Agamben, call each move an |inclusive exclusion| feeding the |anthropological machine| that keeps human-only rule looking like plain common sense. And the stakes aren't academic. States command loyalty and resources because their subjects assume only humans can be sovereign. If God visibly materialized tomorrow, "to whom would people give their loyalty, and could states in their present form survive?"

The UFO, they argue, poses exactly that kind of vertical question. Which is why no state can afford to ask it. The thesis in one line: "The UFO can be 'known' only by not asking what it is."

## A Puzzling Taboo {#puzzling-taboo}

On the night of March 30–31, 1990, two Belgian Air Force F-16s scrambled to intercept a large unidentified object over Brussels. A policeman watched it; ground radars tracked it. The pilots got radar lock three times, never visual contact, and each time the object answered with violent turns and altitude changes estimated at 40g. That's beyond any known aircraft, and beyond what a human pilot could survive. Belgium's defense minister said publicly that he couldn't explain it. The case is still unexplained.

Since 1947, more than 100,000 UFOs have been reported worldwide, many by militaries. Not one of the world's 190-plus states, and not the scientific community either, has made a systematic effort to identify them; no state has even looked for patterns. Officially, the UFO is a |non-object|: "not just unidentified but unseen and thus ignored." And the denial is active, not passive. Ufology gets branded pseudo-science, scientists pay a professional price for curiosity, and states wave off public belief in UFOs as irrational while keeping their own sighting reports secret. UFO denial is political, the authors write, "more like Galileo's ideas were political for the Catholic Church than like the once ridiculed theory of continental drift."

This is the |UFO taboo|.

> **UFO taboo** — A prohibition in the authoritative public sphere on taking UFOs seriously — in the authors' gloss, "thou shalt not try very hard to find out what UFOs are."

The taboo is puzzling three times over. Confirmation of extraterrestrial life would be among the most important events in human history, so investigating even a remote possibility is rational. That's the exact logic that funded |SETI|: "With no evidence whatsoever for such life, why not study UFOs, which are close by and leave evidence?" Second, states subject nearly everything to |securitization| because declared threats expand their powers. Yet not this one. Third, plain old curiosity.

The deeper puzzle is that officials claim to know UFOs are conventional (hoaxes, atmospheric phenomena, instrument error, honest mistakes, secret technology) without ever investigating. This is what Nancy Tuana calls an |epistemology of ignorance|: unknowledge that gets actively produced, "not simply a gap in our knowledge, like the cure for cancer, but something actively reproduced by taboo." Tuana's concept sits inside a wider field with a name of its own, |agnotology|, the study of ignorance that gets manufactured rather than merely found.

> **agnotology** — The study of culturally manufactured ignorance — how doubt and unknowledge get produced on purpose, as in tobacco-industry science and climate denial.

So the paper's question is social-scientific, not ufological. Not what UFOs are, but why the authorities refuse to ask, presupposing only that they might be extraterrestrial.

> **Subguide queued**
>
> Tuana's |epistemology of ignorance|, and the wider field of |agnotology|, is a reusable conceptual tool well beyond UFOs. Manufactured doubt in tobacco science, climate denial, the knowledge-removal dynamics of classified research: all of it runs on the same machinery. Worth a short companion guide of its own.

## The Rules of the Game {#rules-of-the-game}

Here's the argument, compressed. Modern rule runs mostly on |governmentality|, and when something threatening shows up at the limits of that routine management, sovereign power surfaces to decide the exception. The UFO compels exactly that kind of decision. And the decision can't be made, because making it would presuppose the very anthropocentrism the extraterrestrial possibility threatens.

> **governmentality** — Foucault's term for modern rule through the routine management of populations — statistics, procedures, specialized knowledge — rather than through dramatic sovereign commands.

Note what kind of claim this is. "Importantly, our argument here is structural rather than agentic," the authors write. "We are not saying the authorities are hiding The Truth about UFOs, much less that it is ET. We are saying they cannot ask the question."

The argument needs a realist premise: critique only works if you grant the UFO itself the power to affect rational belief. Their foil is Jodi Dean's *Aliens in America*, which reads UFO ignorance as a symptom of postmodernity's collapse of certainty. Dean practices |methodological atheism| toward the phenomenon, and ironically ends up reinforcing the skeptical orthodoxy she set out to analyze. But unlike God, UFOs leave physical traces on radar and film. They're natural phenomena, squarely inside science's jurisdiction. Which produces the paper's striking inversion: "in the UFO context science would be critical theory."

The stance they adopt is |methodological agnosticism| paired with |strategic realism|.

> **methodological agnosticism** — Most sightings have conventional explanations, but for a hard core of perhaps 25 to 30 percent, neither belief nor denial is scientifically justified.

> **strategic realism** — Act as if the UFO is knowable and investigate it, since an agnosticism that never tests itself hardens into dogma.

They concede there's no direct physical evidence, and they set Roswell-style conspiracy aside ("secrecy is a symptom of the UFO taboo, not its heart"). What they point to instead is four forms of indirect physical evidence: ground traces, electromagnetic interference with aircraft, photographs and video, and radar, as in the Belgian case. Science runs on indirect evidence all the time; over 300 extrasolar planets had been discovered indirectly by the time of writing. Add a massive volume of eyewitness reports from pilots, air traffic controllers, astronauts, and astronomers, sometimes radar-corroborated. "Our argument is that UFO ignorance is political rather than scientific." And then the pivot: "The question today is not 'Are UFOs ETs?' but 'Is there enough evidence they might be to warrant systematic study?' By demanding proof of ETs first, skeptics foreclose the question altogether."

## It Can't Be True {#it-cant-be-true}

The real foundation of skepticism toward the |extraterrestrial hypothesis|, the authors argue, is a priori: "It can't be true, therefore it isn't." Four arguments are carrying that weight, and the paper takes them apart one by one.

*We are alone.* The |Rare Earth hypothesis| holds that human-level intelligence is a freak accident of contingent evolution. Complexity theorists counter with a law of increasing complexity: self-organization trends toward complexity, so intelligence might be common. "Either way, today it is simply not known."

*They can't get here.* Light speed caps travel at 186,000 miles per second. At .001 percent of light speed (66,960 miles per hour, beyond current human capability) the nearest star is 4,500 Earth-years away, and near-light travel demands more energy than Earth uses in a year. And yet simulations show the colonization wave-fronts of expanding extraterrestrial civilizations should have reached Earth within 100 million years even on pessimistic assumptions, "barely a blip in cosmic terms." "In short, ETs should be here." That's the |Fermi paradox|: Where are They? And physics keeps opening doors. Wormholes are predicted by relativity, and warp drive ("engineering the vacuum around a spaceship") drew funding through NASA's Breakthrough Propulsion Program. "If humans are imagining them just 300 years from our scientific revolution, what might ETs 3,000 years, much less 3,000,000, from theirs be imagining?"

*They would land on the White House lawn.* Enter the |zoo hypothesis|: Earth as a wildlife preserve, or a norm of letting species discover things for themselves. Even Star Trek's Federation has a non-interference directive. Either way, "debates about ET intentions have no scientific basis."

*We would know.* Any civilization capable of getting here could control what we know about its being here. Meanwhile, "no authority has ever actually looked for UFOs, the effect of which on what is seen should not be under-estimated."

The payoff: "The ETH cannot be rejected without significant risk of |Type II error|" (rejecting an explanation that's true) and the burden shifts to the skeptics. "Far from proving UFO skepticism, science proves its ignorance." The controversy, they conclude, "has been essentially theological, pitting ET believers against unbelievers," with the unbelievers wielding science's authority without its warrant. Since science doesn't actually justify the unbelief, the unbelief's dominance becomes the thing to explain.

## The Sovereign's Bind {#sovereigns-bind}

Here's the paper's core theory, and it starts with the |state of exception|. Carl Schmitt: "Sovereign is he who decides the exception."

> **state of exception** — Carl Schmitt's concept — the sovereign is whoever decides when normal law is suspended in the face of an existential threat.

Sovereignty sits both outside the legal order (founding and suspending it, deciding like a "miracle") and inside it. Normally, a threat at the limits of governmentality's management gets surveyed and securitized; that's what sovereignty is for. The UFO is "the proverbial dog that didn't bark, a potential threat not only un-securitized but never even properly surveyed." No decision was ever made. There was only ignoring.

Why? The UFO's threat is "at once physical, ontological, and metaphysical." Physical: a technology gap, the possibility of conquest. Ontological: even benign visitors would push humanity toward world government, dissolving the plural state system in which sovereign identity is constituted. But neither one explains the taboo. States happily securitize immigrants, pandemics, and terrorists they can't defeat, and a UFO threat would make an ideal cynical pretext for expanding power. The decisive threat is metaphysical. Modern rule rests on unquestioned anthropocentrism, and sovereignty cannot decide an exception to its own metaphysical foundation. "Anthropocentrism will be secure until scientific evidence to the contrary comes along." So the evidence must never be gathered.

The mechanism is Derridean |undecidability|.

> **undecidability** — Derrida's term for something that belongs to both poles of a dichotomy at once and so resists classification, normally compelling a decision one way or the other.

The UFO is undecidable on the object–subject axis, and each pole threatens on its own. As *object*, it confronts science with possible in-principle unknowability: sightings appear random and unsystematic, and some cases (the Belgian 40g turns) seem to violate physics. UFOs "haunt modern sovereignty with the possibility of epistemic failure": "That we might never know what we cannot know about UFOs." Other unknowns got decided. Psi got researched by states, asteroid impacts got securitized, God stays outside science's purview by mutual agreement. Hence one of the paper's sharpest lines: "It is one of the ironies of modern rule that it is far more acceptable today to affirm publicly one's belief in God, for whose existence there is no scientific evidence, than UFOs, the existence of which—whatever they might be—is physically documented."

As *subject*, it's worse. Unlike animals, whose subjectivity sits safely beneath human rule, extraterrestrials might be "literally 'above' human rule, and thus be sovereign deciders in their own right." This is "the sovereignty of the fully alien (non-human) Other," a potential materialization of the metaphysical not unlike the Christian's Second Coming. Because both poles threaten, the result is |meta-undecidability|: "The UFO is both exceptional and not decidable as exception." That leaves the sovereign in |performative insecurity|, operating like psychoanalytic denial: "the sovereign represses the UFO out of fear of what it would reveal about itself." "Just when needed most, on the palisades, the sovereign is nowhere to be found."

## The Machinery of Ignorance {#machinery-of-ignorance}

If the sovereign can't decide, something else has to keep anthropocentrism "known" as fact. That work falls to |governmentality|: scientific-seeming procedures that settle the question without ever asking it. This is "not the conscious work of a vast conspiracy seeking to suppress the truth about UFOs, but the work of countless undirected practices that in the modern world make the UFO 'known' as not-ET." The design problem, as the authors put it, is precise: "what are needed are techniques for making UFOs known without actually trying to find out what they are."

Four techniques do the job. First, authoritative representations: the U.S. Air Force's standing claim that UFOs are "not a national security threat," the portrayal of ufology as pseudo-science, and the sci-fi-ization of the phenomenon in media, which files it under entertainment. Second, official inquiries: the 1969 |Condon Report|, scientific in appearance but in essence a "show trial," systematically deformed by a priori rejection of the extraterrestrial hypothesis. Third, official secrecy, which "removes knowledge" from the public system entirely; whatever states do know never enters the record. Fourth, Foucauldian discipline: from formal attacks on the "paranoid style" of believers down to the everyday dismissal that generates a |spiral of silence| of self-censorship.

> **spiral of silence** — The self-censorship dynamic in which people who suspect their view is taboo stay quiet, making the taboo look like consensus and deepening the silence.

Notice that none of this needs a director. Each practice makes local sense to the people doing it; together they reproduce an ignorance nobody decided on.

> **Design Hook**
>
> Read the four techniques as an architecture of designed ignorance, the inverse of the usual brief. Ridicule does the work of classification; the spiral of silence does the work of censorship; a show-trial inquiry does the work of an answer. A system this well-built for keeping something out of sight can be mapped, audited, and designed against like any other system.

## Resistance {#resistance}

The taboo, the authors argue, has fissures. The UFO keeps showing up, so non-recognition takes perpetual work. Science and the state want different things from knowledge: the state wants a stable |regime of truth|, while science officially presumes that reality gets the last word. And liberalism contradicts itself, producing subjects who "know" UFO belief is absurd while selling itself as the producer of free thinkers.

The fissures show. France, in the endnotes the "only serious potential exception to the taboo," quietly funding case studies since 1977, released 1,600 UFO reports through |GEIPAN| with 25 percent acknowledged as unexplained, and Britain was disclosing files as the paper went to press. "In this context seeing is resistance."

What the authors prescribe is |militant agnosticism|.

> **militant agnosticism** — Agnostic because by modernity's own realist standards neither belief nor denial about UFOs is justified; militant because "only breaking the taboo in public constitutes genuine resistance."

The stance alone isn't enough, though. It needs a systematic science of UFOs behind it. Not case-by-case post-hoc investigation, which can at best show that some cases lack conventional explanations, but active, funded, long-term observation "in which observations are actively sought in order to analyze patterns from which an intelligent presence might be inferred." The catch? Only epistemic authorities have those resources, and they're the parties most invested in the taboo. The costs are real: funding, publication, reputation. "UFO resistance might not be futile but it is certainly dangerous, because it is resistance to modern sovereignty itself."

The close lands on sovereignty's Achilles heel. Rule rests on the capacity to decide threats, and "the UFO threatens humans' capacity to decide those threats, and so cannot be acknowledged without calling modern sovereignty itself into question." The paper's last lines: "after sixty years of modern UFOs, human beings still have no idea what they are, and are not even trying to find out. That should surprise and disturb us all."

> **Territory Bridge**
>
> |militant agnosticism| is, nearly one to one, the lab's own epistemic stance. The lab's position document claims no mechanism for any specific phenomenon, only that the phenomena and the research programs around them are serious enough to warrant work done in public, now, without waiting for disclosure. Wendt and Duvall supply the political theory for why the public part is the active ingredient: a taboo enforced by silence only breaks out loud, which makes the lab's |T4| program (guides, instruments, citizen science) resistance in their technical sense.

## Design Implications {#design-implications}

For designers, the paper's first gift is that it renders ignorance as architecture. The four techniques form a working system: components, interfaces, maintenance routines, failure modes. Most design effort goes into making things visible, legible, usable. Here's a fully built system for keeping something unseen, and Wendt and Duvall have effectively published its schematics. Mapping designed ignorance is the prerequisite to designing its opposite.

The second gift: the paper tells you what undoes the system. Not argument, which stays trapped inside the theological deadlock of believers versus unbelievers. Instrumentation. Their demand for a pattern-seeking observational science reads, fifteen years early, like the founding documents of today's instrumented sky surveys. The resistance the political theorists imagined arrives as hardware. Every calibrated camera array, every open data pipeline, is |militant agnosticism| made physical: public, strategic, and indifferent to ridicule.

There's a smaller, sharper brief hiding inside the |spiral of silence|. Self-censorship is an interface problem as much as a social one. Reporting systems that protect witnesses, aggregate corroboration, and normalize curiosity are designs that interrupt the spiral. Design that widens the fissures, in the authors' terms, is design that makes seeing cheaper than silence.

> **Design Hook**
>
> Build the instrument, not the argument. Case-by-case post-hoc investigation can only ever conclude "unexplained"; the paper's prescription is active, long-term, pattern-seeking observation. Its own endnote 76 names the concrete exemplar: Scot Stride's 2001 JBIS paper on an instrument-based method to search for extraterrestrial robotic probes, a design brief for systematic sky surveys written before the field existed to receive it.
